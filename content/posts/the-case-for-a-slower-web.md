The web I fell for was made of pages. You followed a link, a page arrived, you read it, and then you decided what to do next. The decision was yours. Somewhere along the way the page gave way to the feed, and the feed does not wait for a decision — it simply keeps arriving, and the only choice it leaves you is how long to keep scrolling before you feel slightly worse.

I do not think the answer is nostalgia. The old web was slow in the wrong ways too — slow to load, slow to find, often ugly. What I miss is not the dial-up tone but the *stillness*: a document that knew what it was, sat at one address, and made no attempt to follow me around afterwards.

## What a slow page asks of its maker

A page that means to sit still has to be built differently. It loads quickly because it is mostly text. It does not phone home, because it has nothing to report and no one to report it to. It survives with the scripts turned off, because there were never any scripts. It is, in a quiet way, an act of hospitality: it asks nothing of the reader except that they read.

{{< callout label="In practice" >}}This site is the argument made literal — static HTML and one small stylesheet, no JavaScript, no analytics, no third parties. If a page can be this calm, more of them should be.{{< /callout >}}

None of this requires renouncing the modern web wholesale. It requires only deciding, page by page, what a thing is *for* — and being willing, more often than is fashionable, to let it be small, finished, and content to wait.
