Georgia was drawn to be read on bad screens at small sizes, and that is exactly the test most type still has to pass. It is on nearly every device already, which means it costs the reader nothing to download and never leaves them looking at a blank rectangle while a webfont decides whether to arrive. There is a humility in choosing it that I have come to value.

It is also, quietly, a beautiful face. Old-style numerals that sit down into the line rather than standing to attention. Generous, slightly stout letterforms that hold up when the screen and the eyes are tired. An italic that means it. You can spend a great deal of money and effort reaching for something that reads worse.

There is a small lesson in this that goes beyond type. So much of making things well is not acquisition but attention — working with what is already to hand, and working with it carefully, rather than assuming the answer must be something you do not yet own. Georgia is the typeface I did not have to go and get. Most days, it is more than enough.
