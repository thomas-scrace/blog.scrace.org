The first time through a good book you are mostly finding out what happens. This is a real pleasure, but it is also a kind of noise — suspense crowds the page and leaves little room for anything else. It is only on the second reading, when you already know how it ends, that the sentences finally get your full attention.

What strikes me about re-reading is that the book holds perfectly still while everything on my side moves. The words on the page are exactly the ones that were there ten years ago. The reader is not. A passage I underlined at twenty-five embarrasses me now; one I skimmed past turns out to have been the whole point. The book becomes a fixed instrument for measuring how far you have drifted.

{{< pullquote >}}We do not read the same book twice, because we are not the same reader twice.{{< /pullquote >}}

This is the quiet argument for keeping books rather than clearing them out. A shelf of read books is not a trophy cabinet; it is a set of appointments with later versions of yourself. Some of them you will keep, and find the room changed. That is not the book failing to stay current. That is the book doing the one thing books are uniquely good at — staying still, so that you can see yourself move.
