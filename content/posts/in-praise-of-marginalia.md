For years I read as though the book were a museum exhibit — to be admired, certainly, but never touched. Clean margins, an unbroken spine, no evidence that anyone had been there. I thought this was respect. I have come to think it was a kind of absence.

A pencil changes the relationship entirely. Underline a sentence and you have agreed to be accountable to it. Write *yes* in the margin and you have taken a side; write *really?* and you have started an argument you will have to finish. The book stops being a monologue you sit through and becomes a conversation you are, at last, allowed to answer back to.

The marks are also a letter to a later self. I reopen a book I read a decade ago and find a younger reader arguing with me from the margins — sometimes wiser than I remember being, sometimes wrong in ways I had forgotten I was capable of. Either way, the pencil kept the record that memory did not.

So I have made my peace with spoiling the page. A book that has been read with a pencil is not damaged; it is finished — or rather, it has been let into the only place where reading actually happens, which is the back-and-forth between the sentence and the person holding it.
